==Career==

===''The Source''===
Shecter co-founded<ref name=CoFounSource.TheGradi2021/> ''The Source'' magazine in 1988 in [[Cambridge, MA]] with his roommate [[David Mays]] initially as an effort to promote his rap music radio show on [[WHRB]] 95.3 FM.<!---XTR**<ref name=3Unkut/>-->Often cited as “The Bible of Hip Hop,” the magazine devoted coverage to the rising rap and hip-hop music genres.<!---XTR <ref name=5Androids>{{cite web|last1=Khal |title=The DAD Power 20: The Most Influential People in Dance Music Right Now
|url=http://doandroidsdance.com/features/dad-power-20/s/jonathan-shecter
|website=Do Androids Dance? |access-date=June 15, 2014}}</ref>-->Shecter served as editor-in-chief of ''The Source'',<ref name=Bawtamaw93>{{cite news |newspaper=[[Baltimore Sun]]
   |url=https://www.baltimoresun.com/news/bs-xpm-1993-01-15-1993015007-story.html
   |quote=Shecter, editor of the Source 
   |title=Women rap men in their rap songs |date=January 14, 1993 |access-date=January 16, 2023}}</ref> while Mays handled publishing duties.<!---While still in college, the two hired Harvard Law student James Bernard as senior editor overseeing culture-related content, and Ed Young as the associate publisher.<ref name=3Unkut /> The four men immediately became shareholders in the ownership of the magazine. Inspired by ''[[Rolling Stone]]'', ''[[Spin (magazine)|Spin]]'', ''[[The Village Voice]]'', and ''[[NME]]'' magazine, ''The Source'' carried the original tag line "The Voice of the Rap Music Industry."<ref name=3Unkut /> The magazine originally attracted a younger audience, ages 12 and 13, and featured numerous reoccurring columns such as "Unsigned Hype", reporting on up-and-coming music artists. Throughout its years, ''The Source'' would go on to feature cover stories on the crack cocaine epidemic, police brutality, and New York's investigations of high-profile emcees.-->The magazine's officers relocated to [[New York City]] in 1990 with the intention to expand the magazine into a mainstream market publication;--><!---''The Source'' was re-branded as "Hip-Hop Music, Culture, and Politics", and-->Shecter brought on additional staff.<!--- including Matty C, Reef, Reggie Dennis, and Philadelphia childhood friend Chris "Cee" Wild. The team was known as the "Source Mind Squad."<ref name=3Unkut />--><!---Not long after the relocation to New York,--> Mays secured the magazine's first newsstand distribution agreement, and publication's distribution increased. Funding was via selling ad space, including to rap performer Christopher Wallace, also known as [[Notorious B.I.G.]] and performer/producer [[Sean John Combs]].<!--- contacted Shecter soon after to gather information on the artist with interest in signing him under his label.<ref name=2Pizzo /> Occasionally ''The Source'' received backlash from the artists it covered due to negative reviews. [[Compton, California]]-based west coast hip hop artists [[Rodney-O & Joe Cooley]] openly criticized the magazine in 1993 for a poor review of their album ''Fuck New York''. American hip hop group [[Public Enemy (group)|Public Enemy]] created a music video mocking Shecter and James Bernard after Bernard criticized their music in a recent article.<ref name=3Unkut/> As ''The Source'' became more popular, it started a touring show in New York City called The Source Tour, featuring artists such as [[Cypress Hill]], [[Hit Squad]], [[Redman (rapper)|Redman]], [[K-Solo]], [[Das EFX]], and many others.<ref name=3Unkut/>-->

Shecter left ''The Source'' magazine in 1995, which by then was recognized by ''Audit Bureau of Circulation''.<!--I have it somewhere, found via GOGGLE, to be added B"N in a future edit-->.<!--- as Mays's Boston-based hip hop group [[The Almighty RSO]] and Raymond Scott, also known as [[Benzino]], gained greater influence over the publication.<ref name=2Pizzo />The publication has over eight million subscribers worldwide and remains one of the most popular hip-hop magazines in the world.-->

===Entrepreneur===
